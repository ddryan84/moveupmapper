The housing market in numbers.

Five charts that put today's market in historical perspective — from monthly affordability and rate dynamics to the down payment barrier, opportunity cost, and the lock-in effect that's keeping supply off the market.

Chart 01 — Affordability

The monthly payment crisis

Estimated monthly principal & interest payment on the median U.S. home with 20% down, using the prevailing 30-year fixed rate each year.

Sources: FRED MSPUS (median home sales price), Freddie Mac PMMS (30-year fixed rate). Calculation: 20% down, 360-month amortization.

The double squeeze: From 2020 to 2023, the monthly payment on a median home jumped from roughly $1,150 to over $2,100 — not because prices alone rose, but because rates tripled simultaneously. A buyer in 2023 paid more per month for a similar home than a buyer at the 2006 market peak, despite the same purchase price in nominal terms.

Chart 02 — Rate Dynamics

Why rates stayed high after the Fed cut

30-year fixed mortgage rate vs. 10-year Treasury yield, and the spread between them. The spread is the mortgage market's "risk premium" — and it's unusually wide right now.

Sources: Freddie Mac PMMS (30-year fixed), Federal Reserve H.15 (10-year Treasury constant maturity). Annual averages.

The spread story: Historically, 30-year mortgages price at roughly 1.7 percentage points above the 10-year Treasury. That spread widened to nearly 3 points in 2023 — meaning buyers paid a hidden premium on top of already-elevated Treasury yields. As the spread normalizes, mortgage rates can fall even without Fed action.

Chart 03 — Access Barrier

The 20% down payment as a share of income

A 20% down payment on the median U.S. home, expressed as a percentage of median household income. This is how much of one year's gross income a typical household would need to save.

Sources: FRED MSPUS (median home price), FRED MEHOINUSA672N (real median household income). Down payment = 20% of median price.

What this means: In 2000, saving a 20% down payment required about 79% of one year's median household income. By 2022, that number crossed 120% — meaning the typical household would need to save their entire annual gross income, plus a fifth of another year, just to clear the entry barrier. Incomes have grown; they haven't kept pace.

Chart 04 — Opportunity Cost

What $100 invested in 2000 became

Median home price appreciation indexed to $100 in 2000, compared to the S&P 500 total return and a small-cap value index over the same period. This is the price-appreciation-only comparison — it excludes leverage, shelter value, dividends, taxes, and transaction costs.

Sources: FRED MSPUS (home price appreciation). Equity returns approximate annual total returns (dividends reinvested). Small-cap value based on Fama-French US Small Cap Value factor data. No transaction costs, leverage, or tax effects modeled. Starting point: January 2000.

The framing matters: This chart compares home price appreciation to equity total return. A primary residence also provides shelter value (imputed rent) and most buyers use leverage (a mortgage), which amplifies gains — and losses. This is not an apples-to-apples investment comparison. Chart 05 — Supply Constraint

The golden handcuff: existing mortgage rates

Distribution of outstanding U.S. mortgage balances by interest rate bracket, as of early 2024. Most existing homeowners locked in rates far below today's market — and have little financial incentive to sell and give up that rate.

Source: FHFA/Federal Reserve, estimates based on outstanding mortgage originations by vintage. Approximate distribution as of Q1 2024. Current 30-year fixed rate: ~6.7%.

Why supply is frozen: Roughly 63% of outstanding mortgages carry rates below 4%. A homeowner with a 3% mortgage who sells and buys again faces a payment roughly 60–80% higher on the same loan size — purely from the rate difference. This "lock-in effect" is a primary driver of the inventory shortage that's keeping prices elevated even as affordability deteriorated.

Data & Methodology

All data is sourced from publicly available federal datasets (FRED, FHFA, Freddie Mac PMMS) and updated annually. Home prices use the FRED MSPUS series (median sales price of houses sold). Mortgage rates are annual averages from Freddie Mac's Primary Mortgage Market Survey. Treasury yields are Federal Reserve H.15 constant-maturity rates. Household income uses FRED MEHOINUSA672N.

Equity return data uses approximate annual total returns (price + dividends reinvested). Small-cap value returns are based on Fama-French US Small Cap Value factor data (publicly available via the Ken French Data Library). These are approximations intended for educational context, not investment advice. The opportunity cost chart excludes leverage, transaction costs, taxes, and the shelter value of homeownership — all of which materially affect the actual comparison. Use the Rent vs. Buy calculator for a model that accounts for these variables.
