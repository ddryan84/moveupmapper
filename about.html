About This Site

Purpose, tools, and how the math works

Why This Site Exists

Most people make their biggest financial decisions — buying a home, deciding whether to keep renting, considering a move — with incomplete information. Mortgage calculators show you a monthly payment. Rent vs. buy comparisons flatten complex, multi-year dynamics into a single oversimplified number. And almost none of them show their math.

This site was built to do better. The goal is to give anyone — whether you're a first-time buyer, a long-term renter, or someone weighing a move — a clear, honest picture of what each path actually costs over time, built on real financial math rather than rounded estimates.

Everything here is free. Nothing requires an account. Your inputs never leave your browser.

The Tools

Rent vs. Buy Analysis

The centerpiece of the site. Rather than giving you a single break-even number, this tool models the full financial picture across a custom time horizon of 5 to 30 years.

Monthly Cost Comparison tracks every dollar you'd spend renting versus buying, year by year, across your full horizon. The buying side includes principal and interest, property taxes with annual growth, PMI with automatic drop-off when your loan-to-value ratio reaches 80%, homeowner's insurance, and maintenance costs that scale with your home's appreciated value. The renting side compounds rent at a realistic annual increase. The chart shows exactly where the crossover happens — the year buying becomes cheaper on a monthly basis.

Equity vs. Invested Capital Growth answers the harder question: which path builds more wealth? The equity line tracks the value you've actively built through ownership — principal paid down plus home appreciation above your purchase price. The invested capital line models what happens if a renter invests the same initial capital at a market rate of return, while also reinvesting the annual savings from lower monthly costs. Both scenarios get a fair comparison.

Supporting detail panels break down Year 1 monthly costs by category, show cumulative totals over your full horizon, and display how your amortization split — the ratio of principal to interest — shifts between early and later years of your loan.

Home Swap Calculator

Designed for people weighing a move from one home to another — or modeling a first purchase for the very first time. Enter the details of your current financial position and a prospective home side by side to see a full cost breakdown, including your post-purchase cash position, monthly affordability relative to income benchmarks, and how your buying power shifts over the next decade.

The Affordable Price Range section lets you set a target monthly housing cost and instantly see what purchase price that budget supports — along with an absolute ceiling based on the 36% of income lender threshold and a comparison to your prospective price. The Buying Power Over Time chart shows how income growth, home appreciation, and cost headwinds interact to expand or compress your range over ten years, letting you stress-test both optimistic and conservative scenarios.

First-time buyers and existing owners each get a tailored input mode. Scenario A/B tabs let you run two complete comparisons side by side without losing your work — useful for comparing two properties, two down payment strategies, or a "buy now vs. wait" decision.

How the Math Works

Financial calculators often treat their models as black boxes. This site does not.

Mortgage amortization uses the standard closed-form formula for remaining loan balance at any point in time — the same calculation used by lenders. This means the interest-to-principal ratio is correctly front-loaded in early years, not averaged across the loan.

PMI drop-off is calculated month by month. The tool tracks your declining loan balance alongside your home's appreciated value and identifies the exact month your loan-to-value ratio crosses 80% — at which point PMI is removed from the monthly cost projection. This drop is visible as a labeled callout on the Monthly Cost Comparison chart.

Opportunity cost is applied as a genuine compound return, not a flat addition. The invested capital scenario grows your initial lump sum annually, and each year's cost differential — whether buying costs more or renting costs more — is added to or subtracted from the portfolio and compounds forward. This avoids overstating the renter's investment position.

Maintenance costs are calculated as a percentage of your home's appreciated value each year, not just the original purchase price. This reflects the reality that upkeep costs tend to rise alongside the value and age of the property.

Default values across both tools are calibrated to 2026 national averages — current mortgage rates, median home prices, typical PMI premiums, national property tax averages, and long-run historical returns. Every input can be adjusted to match your specific situation.

Your Data

All calculations happen entirely in your browser. Your inputs are saved in your browser's local storage so your settings persist between visits — but that data is never transmitted to any server, never stored in a database, and never shared with anyone.

This site uses Google Analytics to understand how visitors interact with the tools and displays ads through Google AdSense. Both services may collect anonymized usage data through standard browser cookies. See the Privacy Policy for full details.

A Note on Limitations

These tools are designed to inform, not to decide. Real estate decisions involve factors that no calculator can fully capture: neighborhood trajectory, job stability, local market conditions, the emotional value of ownership, and much more. The numbers this site produces are a starting point for a conversation — ideally one that includes a qualified mortgage professional, financial advisor, or real estate agent who knows your specific situation.
